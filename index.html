The Problem

Calculus didn't invent these solutions.
It revealed why they work.

Every structure in nature is the answer to an optimization problem. The honeycomb maximizes storage and minimizes material. The river meander minimizes energy. The arch minimizes stress. The soap bubble minimizes surface area.

Nature has been solving calculus problems since before calculus existed. What we do today is learn to ask the same questions mathematically — and then go one step further: learn to work backwards from a rate of change to reconstruct what produced it.

Today's connection to the full week: The derivative (Day 2) tells you the rate of change at every point. Optimization uses that derivative to find where the rate equals zero — the peak, the valley, the best possible outcome. That question depends on limits (Day 3). All of it started with algebra (Day 1). Today you see what it was all building toward.

The Honeycomb Conjecture
The Mathematical Question
What is the most efficient way to partition a plane into regions of equal area with the least total perimeter?
The answer is hexagons — but proving it took 2,000 years. Ancient Greeks suspected it. Mathematicians worked on it for millennia. It wasn't formally proved until 1999 by mathematician Thomas Hales.

Bees have been implementing the correct solution for millions of years.

The Calculus Connection
Minimize perimeter subject to equal area — that is a constrained optimization problem. The same structure you will solve in Calculus 1 with derivatives and Lagrange multipliers. Nature found the answer. Calculus explains why it's correct.
